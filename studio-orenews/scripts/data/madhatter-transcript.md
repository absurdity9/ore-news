## Bitcoin's Friction Problem & the Custodian Trap

**Mad Hatter:** Yeah, yes. But I think my perspective was really, I started looking at the past, which was perhaps very nerdy of me, but I started looking at the history and literally every fiat currency has this pattern. The pattern seems to involve, at some point, debasement of the currency, whether it's because of political reasons or something else. They seem to follow a similar pattern.

Obviously Bitcoin, the promise of it was: hey, this is math-based, this is outside the control of somebody to just come in and debase. Now since 2020, my thinking has changed in some ways, primarily because I do think Bitcoin has some structural issues that perhaps will take a longer time to bear out.

In fact, I think Bitcoin is falling into the same trap that, frankly, a lot of fiat currencies have in the past because it doesn't have a good answer for how to solve for friction. So it is hard money, but what we have seen throughout history is whenever there is too much friction -- it's too slow -- custodians step in. And what happens over time is that the custodial network replaces the actual network.

So ask yourself the question: how many people are using the Bitcoin network, the actual network? And the answer is not enough. What is happening is the transactions have shifted to custodians -- whether it is Coinbase, whether it is some other centralized exchange, it doesn't really matter. Whether it's ETFs, they're just different shades of the same story. Ultimately, Bitcoin is falling into the same trap that other large fiat currencies have faced, which is there's too much friction, people fall to custodians, and then custodians control the narrative, the price, the control.

So I think ORE is very interesting to me because it is on top of a highly performant decentralized network. In my mind, a lot of the risk that comes with Bitcoin long-term is resolved just because ORE is on a high-throughput network.

**Brian:** That's a good point. And then of course, if you're a custodian, what's to say you're not making more paper Bitcoin with all the derivative products out there? The perpetual futures and so forth, being able to bet literally on the price of Bitcoin is pretty much taking some of that money that would otherwise go into the network, out of the network, because people are betting on the price movement or doing perpetuals. So all those things are valid.

And the friction -- yeah, you're right. I think the Lightning Network has been more or less a failure over these last few years. There's no adoption. I don't see a Lightning Network. Maybe one or two places I've seen Bitcoin accepted, and that's because I went to conventions. I saw in Denver we had a bar, Bitcoin, I think it was called. You could pay with Bitcoin there, but who would do that? I'd rather just pull out a $10 bill because I don't want to wait there for five minutes for my bill to pay.
