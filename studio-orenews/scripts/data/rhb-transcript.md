## Getting Into Crypto Early (2013)

**RHB:** My name is Raid, and basically I got into crypto around 2013. I dabbled in it when I was in high school at the time. I remember I was purchasing prepaid cards with no names so that I could download Trading 212 and Plus500, which were the apps that I knew back then. It was mostly just trading contracts and derivatives of cryptocurrencies. So it was guesswork mostly -- you had like a 5-second chart and a 10-second chart, and you just say, oh, it's going up or down. That was more gambling than it was anything with fundamentals. I was buying Bitcoin and selling it for like a $10 and $20 profit, and I thought I was making bank at the time. I was 14 years old.

I was in boarding school in Switzerland. My first two or three weeks there, one of the kids that was with me had a Richard Mille, which was about 200K or 300K. And that made me feel like, okay, if he has it, I'm going to get it as well. I'll just have to figure out how to make it. One of the first things I did was think, this trading stuff seems very interesting. That sort of lit the fire of crypto -- starting to see people making crazy amounts of money.

I spent too much time ignoring school, ignoring my day-to-day homework, just trying to find out more about what this stuff is, how it works, how it functions. Then I had to make a decision to step back and focus on what I had at hand. I put everything to the side for about three or four years.

## Return to Crypto (2018)

**RHB:** In 2018, I was in uni in London and I heard a lot of people talking about it -- there was a large increase in price at the time. A couple of friends were mentioning crypto, Bitcoin and so on. I'm like, damn, I remember I had dabbled in this a couple of years ago.

I saw a lot of people that had their Bitcoin from three or four years ago with multiples of X's. I tried to log onto my old accounts, then realized I was just buying short-term contracts, nothing in spot. There was no spot on those platforms. I still have the account history -- I can see that I was buying a Bitcoin for $12 and selling it for $22.

That got me thinking -- Bitcoin had some legs on it and crypto is gaining traction. So what's the next thing coming up? I still remember SBF: "Send me all the SOL you have for $3 and fuck off." I bought a considerable amount at the time, and as it kept going up, I was selling in small portions.

**RHB:** I always had a sort of an ick for ETH. SOL is my game -- quick transactions, no crazy gas fees. It became a cult. It wasn't really just a coin. That's why I pushed more towards the whole Solana ecosystem.

**Brian:** Sol does seem to have the spirit of crypto in it now, feels more cult-like. Ethereum has been so topsy-turvy with their messaging in the last few years. They went from being this fair, just moderator of the crypto space to now pivoting to m'lady-esque stuff. They just made everybody sign a document pledging their allegiance to the Ethereum Foundation -- such a big switch. Solana's had a more straight and true path.

I'd compare it to Hyperliquid -- the Hype team decided to slash down how many HYPE they'd be getting, and as soon as the HYPE is released, they all just stake it. If ETH was done like that today, it would be at a way higher price, but in fact it's the other way around.

## Discovering ORE

**RHB:** One day I came across HardHat's Twitter. A close friend of mine was like, "Oh this looks like an interesting protocol." He was posting about the ORE white paper, the V1 white paper. I was back home, had a couple of months off, so I decided to dig into it.

I had a strong MacBook and back then it was GPU mining. I went through the steps of how to get into the protocol and start mining. It was my first time using CLI on the laptop since I ever started using laptops. My first time downloading the Solana CLI, having to troubleshoot a lot of coding things when I didn't have a background in coding. I have a software-as-a-service company but I have developers that work with me -- I don't do the coding part, I do the entrepreneurship and the management side.

The tools back then, getting into it, making sure I had 100% uptime -- it's a whole different world from what we have today. It has been completely oversimplified so that anyone can come in and start mining. I was explaining to my girlfriend: "This is what ORE is. This is what mining it is. This is what a motherlode is." It became really easy to explain and get people into.

MineMore now -- you can access it through Phantom, through Solflare, through the web, through multiple ways. It has made mining actually very easy.

## CLI vs. Simplified Access

**Brian:** Command line interface is not the way to broaden the reach of any product. Normal people will see a command line and PowerShell and freak out. I'm not a developer, but I had to use CLI when generating Ethereum staking keys. I had 64 ETH going into this tool, hoping I did it right, sending it to a specific address and hoping it sees it. Command line interface with that much value -- it's a butt-clenching 30 minutes refreshing the page. I'm glad ORE doesn't need CLI tools today because that would limit so many people from accessing this protocol.

**RHB:** In the beginning when it was first launched, you could either use the CLI or the website. The website used to lag a lot, had hiccups. You could only mine with one account at a time. If you opened another tab, it would log you out of the first account and create a new one. There was no exporting privately -- very complex. A lot of people were too afraid to put anything into it.

## The Solana Outage

**RHB:** With the CLI, we found that with every different client you could basically have a different miner running. At one point I was pushing maybe a hundred transactions per 20 to 30 seconds. That was when there was the big Solana outage -- all the transaction requests coming in through mining ORE caused the first big shockwaves. Everyone was asking, "What's causing the Solana outage?" and Mert was like, "All these fucking ORE miners need to give us a break."

HardHat was panicking: "Guys, we really need to calm it down." And I'm like, nope -- more miners! I went and got another laptop, opened another screen of 50 miners. People weren't able to make transactions on Solana -- every transaction you'd have to retry ten times because all the block space was taken up by ORE.

Mert was like, "Listen, you need to submit something to Coliseum so that we can actually work together instead of working against each other." Then came ORE V2, which took it down a notch -- it didn't allow the multiple miners and didn't rely on GPU. Now with Firedancer, it doesn't really matter how many transactions go in every minute.

## Early ORE Price Action

I'm enjoying this, getting a lot of people into it.

## MineMore Experience

**RHB:** MineMore basically does what got me addicted to ORE -- running a bunch of different sub-accounts at the same time and keeping them constantly running. That's ideal for me because I don't want to sit down looking at the laptop all day checking that everything is running.

Now we have Spuddy and Wild making sure the server updates are smooth -- miners will be off but turned back on automatically. That reduces a lot of stress. It also makes it so you can get a lot of people hooked onto mining. It's low cost, low risk, high reward -- especially if you hit a nice motherlode like the one two days ago.

**Brian:** Did you land that chase?

**RHB:** I hit it with seven miners. I had 11 running and then three ran out of juice just before, and two of them -- a 10-tile and a 13-tile -- somehow missed it. But having constant notifications, knowing when you hit a top miner, knowing when the motherlode dropped, you have a very nice community constantly talking to each other, telling each other the ML chase is on.

## Mining Strategies

**RHB:** There's activity at both ends of the spectrum. Whenever there's a low ML, you have the cheap hunters deploying a lot of miners at really low deploys, hitting top miners like crazy. Then you have people that chase whenever it gets higher because there's a bigger payout.

There's a fine balance between how much ORE you want to collect or how you want to collect it. You can put a lot of miners going for 10,000 rounds at 0.00001 SOL. Even if it hits one time, you're broken even. Those miners are the most fun -- they happen when you least expect them. When you have really low deploys and it hits, you're like, "Oh, that's fun!" -- then a bunch of people give you fire emojis in the Discord.

**Brian:** MineMore is great for all the tools it gives you. I do wish the front end had tools like the P&L that MineMore lets people see. The front end is more like, "Oh, I got an ORE," but how do you see your P&L over time? You can't on the official front end.

**RHB:** What I haven't managed to understand with the main ORE front end is the no-SOL auto-reclaim. As soon as I found MineMore with the estimated rounds, SOL auto-reclaim, and instant deployment, that was it -- there's really no second best here. They're not doing anything against ORE, but rather a more advanced version of what's already been given. It's a crazy collaboration more than a competition.

**Brian:** I think we're all waiting for ORE to spike and break that trend line.
